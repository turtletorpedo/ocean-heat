Understanding Ocean Heat Content

Ocean heat content measurements track how much heat the ocean has absorbed over time. These measurements are crucial for understanding climate change because:

  • Oceans cover 70% of Earth's surface and absorb more than 93% of the planet's excess heat
  • Heat content is measured from the surface down to 2,000 meters depth
  • Global monitoring networks provide data since the 1950s, showing unprecedented warming
  • OHC data helps predict future climate impacts and validate climate models
  • The ocean's thermal expansion from this heat is the primary driver of sea level rise

This data is collected through coordinated global observation programs and helps scientists track Earth's energy imbalance.

🌊 Sea Level Rise

Thermal expansion from heated oceans is the primary driver of sea level rise, threatening coastal cities worldwide and displacing millions.

🌀 Extreme Weather

Warmer oceans fuel more powerful hurricanes, typhoons, and cyclones. Each storm draws energy from this vast heat reservoir.

🐟 Marine Collapse

Ocean ecosystems are collapsing as temperatures rise beyond what marine life can survive. Coral reefs are dying en masse.

🧊 Ice Sheet Melting

Warm ocean water is melting ice sheets from below, accelerating the loss of Greenland and Antarctic ice at unprecedented rates.

🌡️ Climate Feedback

As oceans warm, they absorb less CO₂ and release more heat back to the atmosphere, accelerating global warming.

⚡ Ocean Currents

Critical ocean currents that regulate global climate are weakening and shifting, threatening to disrupt weather patterns worldwide.
